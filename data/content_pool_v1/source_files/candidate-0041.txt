This overview outlines five architectural tiers for AI agents: simple reflex, model-based reflex, goal-based, utility-based, and learning agents. While these are often presented as layers of complexity, they are distinct methodologies for enabling autonomy. Simple reflex agents operate on immediate condition-action rules, whereas more advanced iterations track world states, simulate future outcomes, or rank preferences through utility functions. Learning agents further enhance this autonomy by refining strategies based on environmental feedback. Central claims:
- AI agents are organized into a taxonomy based on how they process input and make decisions, ranging from simple reactive logic to systems capable of self-improvement.
- Utility-based agents allow for the comparison of multiple possible outcomes using a preference score, a distinction from goal-based models that focus on satisfying a binary condition.
Concepts:
- Simple Reflex Agent: An agent that selects actions based solely on current percepts, without relying on stored past information.
- Model-Based Reflex Agent: An agent that maintains an internal representation of the world's current state to track environmental evolution.
- Goal-Based Agent: An agent that evaluates potential future states based on their ability to satisfy a predefined objective.
- Utility-Based Agent: An agent that selects the most desirable outcome by evaluating the relative preference of different future states.
- Learning Agent: An agent that improves its future performance by evaluating feedback from environmental interactions.
- Multi-Agent System: An environment where multiple independent agents interact to pursue common or individual goals.
